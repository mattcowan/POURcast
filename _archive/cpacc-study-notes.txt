CPACC Notes:
-  It is currently estimated that over 16% of people have disabilities.

MEDICAL MODEL
	- The medical model considers disability as a health problem that affects individual people. It provides cure or management. It does not focus on society's responsibility for creating barriers.

	- Medical treatment is vital for many people with disabilities, and can be lifesaving.

	- The limitation of the medical module is that because it focuses on treating people, it does not address society's role in creating and eliminating barriers.

	Examples: 
	- A person whose lower let is amputated gets a prosthetic that allows them to walk and run.
	- Someone with Type I diabetes uses an insulin pump to better regulate their blood sugar.

SOCIAL MODEL
	- The social model is concerned with equal access. It views society as responsible for creating disability since it creates barriers. And it is society's responsibility to remove those barriers.

	- The social model emphasizes that people with disabilities have a right to participate in society.

	- The model's focus on the disabling conditions in the environment and in society makes it clear that the barriers and challenges experienced by people with disabilities are not inevitable.

	- Weakness: The social model of disability can downplay the biological and medical realities of disability.

	Examples: 
	- A person who uses a wheelchair is able to access all the floors of a building because it was designed to be accessible, with ramps outside and elevators inside.
	- A student with dyslexia can use the audio version of a book instead of having to read it.
	- A screen reader user can fully navigate a website because it was designed to meet the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines.

BIOPHYSICAL MODEL
	- The biopsychosocial model takes into account the physical, psychological, and social contexts in which people with disabilities live. It promotes an approach focused on the unique needs of the individual person, while also addressing systemic barriers and the interplay between a person's disability, their experience, and the environment.

	STRENGTHS
	- The biopsychosocial model promotes wellbeing across different systems and recognizes the importance of social and environmental factors.
	- It promotes social support, which leads to better well-being for society overall.

	WEAKNESS
	- Because the model is more complex than the medical or social models, it can cause confusion about which aspects of someone's experience are due to which factors.

	EXAMPLES
	- An employee with ADHD has a low distraction environment and can relax and focus. This helps them to unleash their talents and be successful.
	- In a Zoom session, the host provides directions about how people can participate, including the option of make themselves visible or not. This recognizes that people have legitimate reasons, including psychological, for participating as they prefer.
	- A patient who was just diagnosed with a life-threatening disease has to find their way to an appointment in a different part of the hospital. They are under a great deal of stress. Because the hospital designed its wayfinding signs to be easily understood, the patient finds their way without having to concentrate too much on it.

HUMAN RIGHTS MODEL
	- The human rights model expands on the social model. It recognizes that people with disabilities have inherent, fundamental human rights that should be protected through legal and policy frameworks.

	STRENGTHS
	- The human rights model is grounded in international human rights law.
	- It promotes the active participation of people with disabilities.

	WEAKNESSES
	- The human rights model does not provide direction on overcoming societal and environmental barriers.
	- It overlooks the experience of disability.

FUNCTIONAL SOLUTIONS MODEL
	- The functional solutions model focuses on practical solutions for individual people. Its goal is to overcome functional limitations using assistive technology or adaptive strategies. It views disability as a practical problem to be solved.

	Most accessibility professionals work within this model!!!

	STRENGTHS
	This module is focused on practical solutions for people with disabilities.

	WEAKNESSES
	Its focus on solutions for individual people limits its use for addressing larger systemic and social issues.

	examples
	A blind person uses a screen reader to access their computer.
	A person with carpal tunnel syndrome uses a split keyboard to reduce pain.
	A person with a spinal cord injury uses a wheelchair to move around.

Social Identity / Cultural Affiliation Model
	This model recognizes that disability may be fundamental to a person's identity.

	Strengths
	- Disability is accepted and can be a point of pride. It can be highly affirming for group members.
	- Groups can also exert political power and act as change advocates.

	Weaknesses
	- People who don't fit the group's expectations can be alienated.
	- Groups also alienate its members from society.

	Example: 
	- Members of the Deaf community consider themselves as part of a culture that includes its own language and customs. (An uppercase D in Deaf is used when referring to this community or its members.)

Economic Model
	- The economic model defines disability by a person’s ability or inability to work, and how much their impairment affects their productivity. It considers financial consequences for them, an employer, and the state. Consequences can include the person's loss of earnings, their costs for assistance, lower profits their employer, and state welfare payments.

	Strengths
	- The economic model recognizes that disability affects a person’s ability to work, and that they may need financial support and/or accommodations.

	Weakness
	- If a person doesn’t meet the legal threshold for disability, or if there is a dispute about their disability, they may not receive the support they need.

	Example
	- A person with a cognitive disability gets paid less than the standard minimum wage.
	- A family qualifies for welfare payments since the parent stays home to take care of their disabled child.

Charity Model
	- The charity model regards people with disabilities as unfortunate and in need of help. It also views people who provide charity as benevolent contributors to a needy population.

	STrengths
	- The charity model can inspire people to contribute their time or resources to provide support to people with disabilities.
	- It can sometimes be the only source of funding for certain needs, for example to study extremely rare diseases.

	Weaknesses:
	- People with disabilities can find this point of view offensive.
	- Charity efforts tend to focus on short-term needs, neglecting more comprehensive and effective long-term solutions.

Disabilities, Barriers, and Solutions
	- Solutions
		- Solutions are practical ways that anyone who is supportive of people with disabilities can ensure better accessibility. Whatever is being designed or offered should be done with consideration, to help people with disabilities access, use, and thrive with it.

	- What are assistive technologies?
		- Assistive technologies are products, devices, systems, or items used by people with disabilities to perform tasks which they could not do otherwise. When they are used with computers, assistive technologies are also called adaptive technologies or adaptive software. Some assistive technologies rely on the output of other “user agents” such as web browsers, text and voice browsers, multimedia players, and plug-ins.

		- Not all assistive technologies are computer-based. For example, communication boards can be made of cardboard. White canes, to-do lists, labels, button hooks, and ear plugs are examples of low technology that are highly effective for various needs.

AUditory Disabilities
	- Deafness (total or near total loss of hearing)
	- many, but not all, people who are deaf know sign language. For those born deaf, often sign language  is their native and most comfortable language. May feel less comfortable reading printed or digital text because it is a second language for them, and phonetic notation does not help comprehension. Those who lose hearing later in life may never learn sign language, and if they do learn it, they may not feel as comfortable speaking in sign and may prefer text.

	Hard of hearing - most hoh people wear hearing aids. Hearing aid or FM system may help.

	466 million people, or 6.1 percent of world population, are deaf or hard of hearing.

	 Captions/Transcripts/Audio Descriptions
	 	Captions
	 		- provide synchonized text description of audio. Generated automatically or entered manually.

	 		Closed captions can be turned on/off

	 		Open captions are burned into the video itself and cannot be removed or turned off

	 		Goal of captions is to provide an experience equivatlent to listening to audio. Captions should include:
	 			- all words spoken, even if offensive
	 			- wordless vocalizations, like laughter, coughs, signs
	 			- important sound effects or environmental sounds

	 	Transcripts
	 		- provide text record of audio, kept in a separate text file outside video player. Transcripts are suitable for recordings of people's speech

	 	Audio Descriptions
	 		- narrate a video recordings visual elements, like characters, costumes, actions, body language, setting, scene changes, and on-screen text. They supplement the regular audio track and are inserted into pauses in the dialogue

	 		- Audio descriptions are especially helpful to people with visual disabilities or who are deaf-blind

	 		- Creating audio descriptions requires special training to write and record a script that is then synchronized with video

	 	- Closed captios usually do not include the audio description.

Speech Disabilities
	- range from mildly slurred speech to physical inability to speak. May be unrelated to their language capabilities, tehy may be able to read, write, and understand. Speech disorders can be caused by underlying disabilities. People may have
		- inconsistent speech errors
		- distorted sounds
		- errors in tone, stress, or rhythm

	Organic Speech Sound Disorders
		- can be developmental or acquired. 
			- Motor/Neurological
				- Apraxia: occurs when brain has difficulty planning movements needed to speak.
				- Dysarthria - occurs when person has difficulty controlling their speech muscles.

			Structural
				- Orofacial abnormalities, such as cleft palatte
				- Strutural problems due to trauma or surgery

			Sensory/perceptual
				- Hearing Loss

		Functional Speech Sound Disorders
			- no known cause, include problems in:
				- Articulation (errors producing clear & distinct sounds)
				- Phonology (errors in correctly using sound patterns)

		Aphasia
			- language disorder resulting from neuroligac damage, including head trauma, brain tumor, infection, stroke, affects all use of language, not just speech, affects a person's abilitiy to produce or understand speech, including ability to read or write, may omit words, be unable to form sentences, or say what they mean. May also not recognize words or understand what is being said.

		No speech
			- mutism, can be caused by damage to brain and/or speech muscles, by emotional or physchological reasons, or combination of causes.

			Neurogenic mutism is caused by brain injury, often the outcome of extreme forms of other organic speech or language disorders, including aphasia, apraxia, dysarthria.

			Psychogenic mutism occurs when speech loss has psychological causes. Three types of psychogenic mutism:
				- Elective mutism, person chooses not to speak.
				- Selective mutism, person wants to but cannot speak due to anxiety
				- Total mutism, person does not speak at all

		Data highly varied, no statistics.

		Common barriers for speech disabilities:
			- Not enough time to communicate, access info, or respond
			- Lack of alternatives for speech communication
			- Other people not indicating that they need clarification of what a person has just said.

	Mobility, Flexibility, Body Structure Disabilities
		Mobility impairment includes:
			- people with upper or lower limb loss or disability
			- challenges with manual dexterity
			- disability in co-ordination with different organs of the body
			- broken skeletal structure

			Physical and mobility impairments cause limitations in the independent, purposeful physical movement of a person's body or of one or more of their extremeties (may be temporary or permanent)

			Disability in mobility can be congenital, acquired with age, or be the consequence of or caused by disease

		Manual dexterity/fine motor control
			- fine motor skills are intricate and detailed movements of the hand and wrist needed to manipulate, control and use objects, produce neat, legible handwriting, and dress independently. 

			- fine motor skills involve the coordinated effors of brain and muscles, built on gross motor skills that allow us to make bigger movements. ppl with fine motor disabilities may have difficulty with:
				- shoelaces, buttons or zippers
				- writing and drawing
				- using a keyboard
				- picking up or manipulating small objects
				- using both hands at the same time

		Ambulation
			- ability to walk
			- perrson may be missing one or both limbs. Or they may have a permanently impaired or unsteady gait making it impossible or impractical to walk
			- impairments may be caused by congenital conditions, disease, or injury, such as cerebral palsy, neuromuscular disorders, amputation, arthritis, and back injuries. People with abulation disabilities need to use an assistive device

		Muscle fatigue
			- non specific symptom characterized by progressive weakness and loss of muscle mass. Can include overwhelming sense of tiredness, lack of energy and feeling of exhaustion, soreness, and it relates to difficulty in performing voluntary tasks.

			Muscle fatigue can occur in any part of the body. People may experience extreme tiredness or feelings of exhaustion, weakness, pain, twitching, trembling, a weak grip, or cramps.


		Body Size/Shape
			- Body size/shape disabilities caused by a variety of disorders that affect a person's stature, proportions, or shape. Examples include acromegaly, dwarfism, rheumatoid arthritis, and obesity.
			- Orthopedit conditions, such as arthritis and joint mobility, can be the underlying cause. Other causes or co-occuring conditions include muscle weakness and fatigue, hearing loss, vision loss, cardiopulmonary disorders, and diabetes.

		Demographics
			- 11% of adults in US have mobility disabilities. Similar figures reported in Europe and Canada.

		Common Barriers

	Ergonomic design
		- The design of products, systems, and environments so they fit people. When ergonomics include the needs of people with physical limitations in their design, it allows such people greater access and use. Examples include:
			- bathtub with door allowing the user to step inside without having to step over the side
			- computer mouse designed vertically rather than horizontally
			- tabs on buttons and bottle cap to make them easier to open.

			- non contact elecronic control would be an exmple of ergonomic design.
			- lots of environmental fixes like handrails, etc. are ergonomic design

	Cognitive disabilities
		- Disabilities that affect a person's cognitive processes are common and frequently hidden from view. Vary from mild to so profound that the affected person needs a great degree of help with daily living. Can be genetic and occur on their own. Can result from a variety of conditions or injuries such as traumatic brain injury.

		- cognitive processes are affected by both internal and external factors. Person's ability to take in and understand information in a certain situation depends on person's capacity as well as context.

		Internal factors include impairments in one or more mental functions.

		External factors include issues such as information overload, stress, sleep deprivation. Studies show stress has a highly disruptive impact on working memory for people with and without cognitive disabilites.

		Intellectual Disability
		- includes significant limitations in intellectual functioning (reasoning, learning, problem-solving) and adaptive behavior, covering a range of social and practical skills.
		Intellectual disability criteria:
			- IQ below 70-75
			- Impairments in adaptive behavior, including skills needed to independtly manage daily tasks, social skills, communication skills, and skills needed to manage school or work.
			- Condition manifests itself in childhood.

			- estimated that 1-3% of global population, or about 200 million people, have intellectual disability.

		Reading & Dyslexia
			- Reading disabilities can include inability to perceive text or process meaning of words, phrases, and ideas. This disability may be the result of congenital difference, injury, delayed development, neurological or physical disability. Some specific reading disabilities have been identified and are regcognized by professionals by diagnosis, such as dyslexia. Often the diagnosis of a learning disability will include components of a reading impairment.

			People who have dyslexia typically read at levels significantly lower than expected. Although the disorder varies from person to person, common characteristics include difficulty reading, phonological processing (the manipulation of sounds), spelling, and / or rapid visual-verbal responding. Recent studies have identified genes that may predispose someone to develop dyslexia.

			Dyslexia is considered one of the most common language-based learning disabilities. It is the most common cause of reading, writing, and spelling difficulties. Of people with reading difficulties, 70-80% are likely to have some form of dyslexia. It is estimated that between 5-10% of the population has dyslexia, but this number can also be as high as 17%.

		Dysgraphia

			Dysgraphia is difficulty with handwriting and other fine motor skills, and can include difficulty expressing oneself in written language or in drawing.

			Its prevalence is unknown.

		Dyscalculia
			Math and computational disability is known as dyscalculia. It impairs a person’s ability to understand communicate mathematics. Dyscalculia may be congenital or result from an injury, disease, or aging.

			Common signs of dyscalculia include:
				- Trouble grasping the meaning of quantities or concepts such as biggest vs smallest.
				- Understanding that the number 5 is the same as the word five, and that these both mean five items.
				- Remembering math facts, such as times tables.
				- Counting money or making change.
				- Estimating time.
				- Judging speed or distance.
				- Understanding the logic behind math.
				- Difficulty remembering numbers while solving problems.

				Demographics
				- An estimated 3–6% of people have dyscalculia.

		Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder
			ADHD is a developmental problem characterized by inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. Children with ADHD are easily distracted by sights and sounds in their environment. Symptoms usually appear by age 7. While people do not outgrow this condition, they can learn to adapt.

			The main symptoms of ADHD are:

			- Inattention
			- Distractibility
			- Difficulty starting, organizing and finishing tasks
			- Being forgetful
			- Feeling restless and having difficulty controlling the feeling
			- Difficulty staying seated
			- Fidgeting
			- Difficulty waiting for one's turn
			- Talking too much
			- Interrupting
			- Demographics
			Prevalence among children is estimated between 2 and 7%, and in adults around 4%.

	Autism 
		According to the World Health Organization, Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) refers to a range of complex brain developmental conditions. Common characteristics include impaired social behavior, communication, and language, a narrow range of interests, and repetitive behaviors.

		ASD begins in childhood and tend to persist into adolescence and adulthood. In most cases, the conditions are apparent during the first 5 years of life. Individuals with ASD often present other co-occurring conditions, including epilepsy, depression, anxiety, and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). The level of intellectual functioning in individuals with ASD is extremely variable.

		People with ASD may have:

		- Sensory issues such as sensitivity to sound, smell, and light.
		- Difficulty with verbal and non-verbal communication, such as in facial expressions, gestures and language, and understanding non-literal expressions.
		- Challenges in social interactions, such as recognizing and expressing emotions. People with ASD may feel overwhelmed in social situations.
		Demographics
		- Approximately one in 100 people have ASD, and its prevalence is increasing.

	Neurological Disabilities
		- Neurological disabilities are impairments of the brain, spine, and the nerves that connect them. Several common ones are presented here.

		Migraine
			- Migraine disease is the world's second most common disability (after back pain). It was previously believed that migraine was caused by blood vessel dilation. The most current understanding is that a primary neuronal dysfunction causes changes both within and outside the brain. Recent research has shown that calcitonin-gene-related-peptides (CGRPs) play an important role in migraine, and several CGRP drugs have been developed to prevent and treat migraine. The disease is complex and not fully understood, and research is ongoing. 

			- A number of factors can trigger migraine attacks, such as hormonal changes, alcohol, bright or flashing light, sleep changes, physical exertion, weather changes, certain medicines and foods, and others.

			Migraine attacks include four phases:

			- Prodrome: Signs that a migraine is going to occur, such as excessive yawning, mood changes, and food cravings
			- Aura: Occurs for some people. Can include visual disturbance or vision loss, difficulty speaking, weakness or numbness 
			- Attack: Pain usually on one side of the head, sensory sensitivities, nauseau or vomiting. Can last for hours or days, especially if untreated
			- Postdrome: Usually lasts a day, and can include fatigue, confusion, and for people, elation
			Demographics
			- It is estimated that 14 - 15% of people globally have migraine disease.

		Stroke
			- A stroke occurs when there is an injury to the brain from a blood vessel that is blocked or has burst. When a person survives a stroke, they can recover completely or can experience lasting brain damage and disability. A stroke requires emergency care, where the goal of treatment is to avoid another stroke.

			Common symptoms include:

			- Aphasia (inability to understand or produce speech)
			- Sudden confusion
			- Sudden severe headache
			- Difficulty seeing, in one or both eyes
			- Sudden numbness or weakness in the face, arm, or leg
			- Loss of coordination
			Demographics
			- Annually, about 15 million people globally experience a stroke.

		Vestibular Disorders

			- Vestibular disorders are problems with the vestibular structures within a person's inner ear or with their central nervous system. Typical symptoms are dizziness and vertigo. Some common causes include aging, injury, inflammation, and other neurological conditions. The odds of vestibular dysfunction increase with age.

			Demographics
			- Annually, between 15% - 20% of adults are affected by dizziness or vertigo.

		Cerebral Palsy
			Cerebral palsy (CP) is a group of disorders that affect nervous system functions such as movement, learning, hearing, seeing, and thinking. It is caused by injury to the developing brain.

			Demographics
			- Globally, about 17 million people have some form of cerebral palsy.

		Multiple Sclerosis
			- MS is a central nervous system disease, in which a person's immune system attacks the coating of their nerves. The breakdown causes communication problems between their brain and their body. The nerve coating damage can be permanent.

			Common symptoms include:

			- blurry vision
			- cognitive problems
			- limb weakness, tingling, or numbness
			- unsteady gait
			- trouble speaking

			Demographics
			- Globally, approximately 2.8 million people are estimated to live with MS.

		Seizure
			Seizure
			- A seizure is a sudden, uncontrolled electrical disturbance in the brain. It can cause changes in behavior, movements, feelings, and consciousness. - If a person has two or more seizures or a tendency to have recurrent seizures, they have epilepsy.

			- Seizures can range from mild to severe, including loss of consciousness. They usually begin in early childhood or late adulthood.

			Depending on the type, symptoms during a seizure can include:

			- Visual hallucinations
			- An inability to speak
			- Convulsions
			- Falling down
			- Biting the tongue
			- Loss of control of the bladder or bowels

			Demographics
			Worldwide, about 65 million people (or 1 in 26) have seizure disorders.

		Photosensitive Epilepsy
			- Photosensitive epilepsy is a condition in which flashing or flickering lights or patterns cause seizures.

			- Different people will be affected by lights at different flash or flicker rates. Lights that flash or flicker between 16 and 25 times a second are the most likely to trigger seizures. But some people are sensitive to rates as low as 3 or as high as 60 a second.

			- Different people may be affected by different types of patterns. Patterns with a high contrast or that appear to move are more likely to trigger seizures. Video games, immersive amusement rides, and virtual reality applications are problematic.

			Demographics
			- About 5% of people with epilepsy have photosensitive epilepsy.

			Please Note!
			- People with other conditions, such as migraine and vestibular disorders, can also be triggered by flash, flicker, and patterns. It is best to simply avoid using such content.

		Common Barriers for Neurological Disabilities:
			- Technologies and spaces that present flashing, flicker, or patterns.
			- Lack of a space to have or recover from a migraine or seizure.
			- Overly bright lights, that frequently characterize public spaces.
			- Lack of availability of emergency care for strokes.
			- Under- and misdiagnosis of migraine.
			- Inaccessible environments for people who use mobility devices.

		Neurological Disability Solutions
			Environmental Improvements
				Physical
					- In spaces that are over lit, lower the lighting to a comfortable level.
					- Avoid flashing or flickering lights.
					- Avoid patterns that give the illusion of movement.
					- Avoid loud sounds.
					- Provide quiet, private space for seizures or migraines.
				Digital
					- Do not require computer games or VR for participation.
					- Avoid flashing or flickering lights.
					- Avoid patterns that give the illusion of movement.
					- Avoid loud sounds.

			Assistive Technologies
				- Anti-glare glasses
				- Ear plugs
				- Supportive gear and protective wear in case of falls
				- Flicker-free monitors
				- E ink monitors
				- Green lights
				- Seizure detection devices
				- Service animals



Psychological Disorders
	- Psychological disabilities include impairments in a person’s perceptions, thoughts, feelings, mood, and behavior. These conditions can be occasional or long-lasting.

	- Anxiety Disorders
	- According to the US National Institute of Mental Health, occasional anxiety is a normal part of life. Many people worry about things such as health, money, or family problems.

	- Anxiety disorders involve more than temporary worry or fear. For people with an anxiety disorder, the anxiety does not go away and can get worse over time. The symptoms can interfere with daily activities such as job performance, schoolwork, and relationships.

	Generalized Anxiety Disorder
		- Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) usually involves a persistent feeling of anxiety or dread that can interfere with daily life. It is not the same as occasionally worrying about things or experiencing anxiety due to stressful life events. People living with GAD experience frequent anxiety for months, if not years.

		According to the US National Institute of Mental Health, symptoms include:

		- Being easily fatigued

		- People with panic disorder have frequent and unexpected panic attacks.

		- Panic attacks are sudden periods of intense fear, discomfort, or sense of losing control even when there is no clear danger or trigger. Not everyone who experiences a panic attack will develop panic disorder.
		
		 During a panic attack, a person may experience:

			- Sweating

For people with social anxiety disorder, the fear of social situations may feel so intense that it seems beyond their control. For some people, this fear may get in the way of going to work, attending school, or doing everyday things.

		Symptoms of social anxiety include:

	Mood Disorders
		- Mood disorders affect a person’s emotional state. Emotions may fluctuate between extreme sadness and extreme happiness or there may be prolonged periods of sadness and loss of interest in activities that were previously enjoyable.
		- The most common mood disorders are depression, bipolar disorder, seasonal affective disorder, and self-harm.

		Depression
			- Depression includes symptoms of lowered mood or a loss of interest for at least 2 weeks, that affects daily activities. Depression is common and can affect anyone. Some common symptoms include:

				- Feeling sad, worthless, or empty

				- A change in eating habits, or sudden weight changes

		Bipolar Disorder

			According to the UK National Health Service, bipolar disorder is a mental health condition characterized by extreme mood swings.

			A person may experience episodes of depression and low energy and episodes of mania where they feel high and overactive. In contrast to ordinary mood swings, each episode of extreme mood may last for weeks. Both types of episodes may involve delusions, hallucinations and disturbed or illogical thinking.

			Symptoms of depression include:

			- Lack of energy
			- Feeling sad, hopeless, or irritable

			- Difficulty sleeping

			Symptoms of mania may include:

			- Feeling full of energy, with great new ideas and important plans
			- Feeling very happy or overjoyed
			- Being easily distracted or agitated

	What are Accomodations???
		Accommodations are modifications to goods, services, or environments so that a person with a disability has equal access and can perform the same tasks as a person without a disability. They are individualized to the person and the situation.

		Accommodations are protected by legislation, which varies by country. Formal accommodations require documentation from a licensed professional trained to diagnose a person's disability.

		In general, an accommodation is not required if it places an undue burden or hardship on the organization being asked to provide it. If an accommodation poses significant difficulty or expense, or if it requires a fundamental alteration to the essential nature of the goods, service, or environment, then the organization does not need to provide an accommodation.



		Examples in Education
			Supports or services that help a student access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning:
			- Extended time
			- Being seated near the teacher
			- Captioning or ASL interpretation
			- A reader, scribe, or word processor for exams
			- Materials in digital format, large print, or braille
			- Calculators
			- Assistive technologies

		Examples in Workforce:
			Supports or services that allow someone to apply for and perform a job or enjoy equal access to its benefits: 
			- A ramp or elevator
			- Lowering or raising a desk
			- Materials in digital format, large print, or braille
			- Assistive technologies
			- Captions or sign language interpreters
			- Service animals
			- Adjusted schedule
			- Working from home

		Undue burden/hardship
			- Significant diffulty or expense for organization being asked to provide accomodation

		Fundamental Alteration
			- accomodation so significant that it alters the essential nature of the goods, services, or environment

		More than half of accomodations for disability cost nothing, and a typical one-time expenditure may equal $500

	Demographics
		People with disabilities are the world's largest minority group. According to the World Health Organization:

		- An estimated 1.3 billion people experience significant disability. This represents 16% of the world’s population, or 1 in 6 of us.
		- Persons with disabilities have twice the risk of developing conditions such as depression, asthma, diabetes, stroke, obesity or poor oral health.
		- Persons with disabilities face many health inequities.
		- Persons with disabilities find inaccessible and unaffordable transportation 15 times more difficult than for those without disabilities.
		- Across the world, people with disabilities have poorer health outcomes, lower education achievement, less economic participation and higher rates of poverty than people without disabilities. This is partly because people with disabilities experience barriers in accessing services, including:

			- healthcare
			- education
			- employment
			- transportation
			- information

		These difficulties are exacerbated in disadvantaged communities and those that are in conflict zones or are otherwise unsafe.

		According to the United Nations Fact Sheet on Persons with Disabilities:

		- 80% of people with disabilities live in developing countries.
		- In most OECD (Organisation for Econonomic Co-Operation and Development) countries, the incidence of disability is greater for women than for men.
		- 90% of children with disabilities in developing countries do not attend school.
		- The global literacy rate for adults with disabilities is as low as 3% and 1% for women with disabilities.
		- Students with disabilities in higher education remain under-represented, although their numbers are rising.
		- Unemployment among persons with disabilities is as high as 80% in some countries.

		- Statistical data on disability is collected and categorized in different ways, so it is important to understand each study within its context.

		There are also general limitations in the collection of statistical data on disabilities:

		- Details about different disabilities are usually not included in census surveys.
		- Disability terminology varies between countries, which makes it difficult to compare as conditions may be defined differently.
		- Many people with disabilities have more than one disability and end up between defined groups.

		Keep these factors in mind when analyzing and communicating statistics on disability. Despite these limitations, disability statistics can be useful as background information for interventions or for giving a sense of the scale of a specific issue.

		Inclusive Language
			- Understanding other people's perspectives and being sensitive to language around disability demonstrates respect for people's dignity. As awareness and inclusion improve, terminology evolves. It is important to avoid terms which are offensive and outdated, and to know whether someone with a disability prefers person-first or disability-first language

			The more common recommendation is to use people-first language. Doing so emphasizes the person, not the disability. For example, using the phrase “a person with a disability”, instead of “a disabled person," is considered respectful.

			People-first language is the form used in the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities. It is also used in communications by G3ICT/IAAP in line with the UNCRPD.

			Examples: 
			- A person with / has a disability
			- Someone who uses a wheelchair

		Identity-First Language
			- Some people with disabilities prefer to use identity-first, in this case their disability, language to refer to themselves. By placing their disability first, a person is indicating that it is a key part of their identity. Identity-first language is often used by self-advocates within disability communities.

			Example Identity-First Terms:
			- A disabled person
			- A Deaf person (Deaf with a capital D indicates that a person identifies themself as part of the Deaf culture)

			gENERAL gUIDELINES
			- DO:
				- Speak directly to the person.
				- Offer assistance only if you have asked permission and it is accepted, and provide the help in the way the person asks you to — and don't be offended by a refusal.
				- Treat people as individuals and treat adults as adults.
				- Acknowledge someone's ability to make judgments and decisions on their own behalf.
				- Follow a disabled person’s lead about discussing their disability; if they offer a detail, you can inquire.
				- Do your best to interact with the person, not their disability.

			- DON'T:
				- Don’t speak to someone's assistant or service animal.
				- Don’t make assumptions that someone wants your help, or take action without their request or acceptance.
				- Treat people as individuals and treat adults as adults.	
				- Don’t make assumptions about what someone can or cannot do.
				- Don't assume someone isn't capable.
				- Don't ask personal or medical questions, such as if they were born with a disability or any other personal details, unless they bring it up first.
				- Don't worry about making mistakes — just ask.

		Interacting with People with Mobility Disabilities:
			Do:
			 - ask someone if they prefer that you sit or stand
			 - Keep paths free of barriers

			Don't:
				- never touch or move someone's wheelchair or assistive device without permission. Such devices are considered extensions of a person's body
				- Don't speak to someone's assistant instead of directly to them.

		Interacting with people with visual disabilities:
		DO:
		- tell them who you are, introduce other people who are there, and say where they are
		- ask them if they wish to be warned about steps, doors, other obstacles
		- Say clearly where their seat is
		- Feel free to use common sayings such as 'see you tomorrow'

		Don't:
		- don't grab a person to guid them - let them take your arm
		- Don't touch service animal without permission
		- don't rely on facial expressions or gestures as a major part of your interaction
		- don't speak to someone's assistant instead of them

		Interacting w/People with Hearing or Speech Disabilities
		Do:
		 - Position yourself in their vision and attract their attention with light touch or wave if needed
		 - ask person how they want to communicate, they may wish to lip-read, for example
		 - Speak at normal pace and face the light
		 - Be patient with people with speech impairments
		 - Ask them to repeat if necessary, and tell them what you have understood so far

		 Don't:
		 - Don't shout at people who are d/Deaf or have a hearing impairment
		 - Don't assume they cannot interact with you
		 - Don't cover your mouth or turn away while speaking
		 - Don't correct them or finish their sentences
		 - If you don't understand, don't pretend you do

		 Interacting with People with cognitive and neurological disabilities.
		 Do:
		 	- for cognitive, use language that is clear and concrete, give them time to respond
		 	- if a person has a seizure lasting more than 5 minutes, call ambulance
		 	- If someone just had a seizure, give them time alone afterward, as they may be disoriented or embarrassed

		 Don't:
		 	- don't be impatient. Repeat, speak more slowly, clarify as needed
		 	- don't restrain their movements, and don't move the person
		 	- Don't give anything to eat or drink until the person is recovered

		 	- Acknowledge the person's feelings and express care and concern

User Centered Design		 	
	User-centered design is an iterative process that puts people at the center of the design of products, services, and environments. (For convenience, we'll shorten the terms to "products.") The key goal of user-centered design is to understand users and the context in which they will use the product (such as in low light or sitting down), in order to create products that are highly usable and accessible.

	Designers first research potential users of the product and any requirements, then design and test solutions. Test feedback becomes another type of research, as results are incorporated into the next design. The process continues until the product design is complete.

	The four phases of user-centered design include:

	- Understand the users and their context.
	- Identify user and business requirements.
	- Design a solution.
	- Evaluate the solution against the context and requirements.

	Products should be usable by everyone
		Every product, service, or environment should be usable by people with disabilities. The most effective ways to create accessible products are to:

		Include people with disabilities in every phase of requirements gathering, design, development, and testing.

		Follow standards:
		- Websites and other information communication technology should meet the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, and the built environment should meet ISO 21542.

	How to integrate accessibility into design
		- Incorporate real (not theoretical) people in the design.
		- Ensure everyone on a project understand the needs of people with disabilities.
		- Include users with disabilities throughout the design process.
		- Have users with disabilities evaluate accessibility.


	Usability
		Usability focuses on ease of use. Designers focus on three aspects:

		- An interface should be easy to use.
		- The design should make it easy for people to achieve their goal.
		- An interface should be easy to learn so using it again is even easier.
		- A highly usable product reduces cognitive load, which is the effort needed to think. Users should be able to achieve their goals quickly and without frustration or error.

		Some ways of reducing cognitive load include:

		- Using clear language.
		- Providing only as much information as needed.
		- Eliminating distractions.
		- Simplifying the environment or user interface.
		- Conducting user research to understand the target audience.
		- Following accessibility and universal design standards.
		- Accessibility is central to usability, since products should be designed to be as easy as possible for ALL the people who want to use them.

	User Experience
		User experience is concerned with the entire journey a person has with a product. It addresses every point of interaction a person has with it over time, including:

		- Becoming aware of the product.
		- Acquiring or accessing it.
		- Using it for the first time.
		- Using it subsequent times.
		- Their feelings about it.

	Benefits of Accessibility:
		Accessibility enables people with disabilities to participate in society, in major life activities such as education and work, and in social activities that are needed for health and happiness.

		Families, communities, and society benefit from the increased independence of more people, the contributions of people with a variety of abilities and disabilities, cost savings, and improvements to the built and digital world that increase access and usability for everyone. Schools, employers, groups, and organizations of all types also benefit from the increase in innovation and improvements to problem-solving brought by diversity through inclusion.

		Benefits for People with Disabilities and their Families
			- People are not isolated or hidden from society.
			- Inclusion allows people with disabilities to participate in society and be valued as full citizens.
			- Increased opportunities for employment lead to greater family income and wealth potential.
			- Access to child care and education creates more opportunities and helps break the cycle of poverty.

				Increased independence strengthens society

Globally, the extended market is estimated to have $6 trillion in annual disposable income. According to Microsoft, as accessibility features are included [in software], overall customer satisfaction improves.

				For customers with disabilities, accessibility improvements are essential for equal access. However, accessibility provided options are useful to all customers in various situations. For example:
					- People using mobile phones, smartwatches, smart TVs, and other devices with small screens and different input modes.
					- People with temporary disabilities such as a broken arm or lost glasses.
					- Challenging situations such as bright sunlight or noisy environments.

			Accessibility Minimizes Legal Risk
				As web use is woven into modern life all over the world, governments and regulators have mandated laws and policies that strengthen the rights of people with disabilities to participate in online digital information and services.

				With legal risks increasing, smart businesses, particularly those with global activities, are creating accessibility policies and programs to mitigate risk to protect both their assets and their reputations.

	Web Accessibility
		The power of the Web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect.
		- Tim Berners-Lee, W3C Director and inventor of the World Wide Web

		Web accessibility is the ability of a website to be easily navigated, used, and understood by people with disabilities. It combines web development and file creation standards with universal design practices.

		According to the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) Web Accessibility Initiative's Introduction to Web Accessibility:

		Web accessibility means that websites, tools, and technologies are designed and developed so that people with disabilities can use them. More specifically, people must be able to perceive, understand, navigate, interact with, and contribute to the Web.

	Other benefits of web accessibility
		People without disabilities
			Web accessibility also benefits people without disabilities, including:

			- People using mobile phones and other devices with small screens and different input modes.
			- Older people.
			- People with temporary disabilities.
			- People with situational limitations such as in bright sunlight.
			- People using a slow Internet connection, or who have limited or expensive bandwidth.

		Website Discoverability
			Websites that meet the WCAG guidelines make themselves more easy to be found by search engines. There is overlap between the guidelines and SEO (search engine optimization) best practices. For example:

			- Accurate page titles help people who use screen readers and describes the content to search engines.
			- Captions are critical for people who are deaf or hard of hearing. Since the captions are text, they can be crawled by search engines.

			- Good color contrast makes text and images easier to see.
			- Adequate spacing between lines of text increases readability.
			- Plain language is easier to understand.


	WCAG Overview and Structure
		The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (frequently referred to as WCAG, pronounced "WICK-ag") are an internationally accepted set of technical standards that explain how to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities.

		The standards are created by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) through its Web Accessibility Initiative. They are intended for web and application developers and designers, content creators, policymakers, purchasing agents, teachers, and students.

		The most current version is WCAG 2.2.

		WCAG 2.0 remains approved as ISO standard ISO/IEC 40500.

		The WCAG standards are organized into:

		- Principles
		- Guidelines
		- Testable success criteria

	WCAG Principles:
	 	Web pages and applications must be:

		- Perceivable (Available to a person's senses - sight, hearing, and / or touch)
		- Operable (User interface components must work for people with disabilities)
		- Understandable (The interface and content must be understandable)
		- Robust (The content must be able to be interpreted by other user agents, eg web browsers, including assistive technologies)
		You can remember the principles by the acronym: POUR.

	WCAG Guidelines

		Each principle is broken down into a number of guidelines. The guidelines are detailed and include testable success criteria.

		For example, under the Perceivable principle, Guideline 1.1 requires providing text alternatives for non-text content, such as images and image buttons. This is needed so that people with visual, cognitive or other impairments and who use screen reading technology can perceive the content and function of such images.

		High quality alternative text describes an image in context, making it meaningful for the person reading the text alternative.

	3 Levels of Success Criteria
		Each guideline includes success criteria, which are categorized into three levels:

		A: Minimal accessibility.
		AA ("double A"): Accessibility for most people. Most commonly used.
		AAA ("triple A"): Highest level of accessibility.

	WCAG Principle: Perceivable
		Information and user interface components must be presented to users in ways they can perceive. In other words, it cannot be invisible to all of their senses.

		- Provide text alternatives for any non-text content.
		- Provide captions and other alternatives for multimedia.
		- Create content that can be presented in different ways, including by assistive technologies, without losing meaning.
		- Make it easier for users to see and hear content.

	WCAG Principle: Operable
		Users must be able to operate the interface. The interface cannot require interaction that a user cannot perform.

		- Make all functionality available from a keyboard.
		- Give users enough time to read and use content.
		- Do not use content that causes seizures or physical reactions.
		- Help users navigate and find content.
		- Make it easier to use inputs other than keyboard.

	WCAG Principle: Understandable
		Users must be able to understand the information as well as the operation of the user interface. The content or operation cannot be beyond their understanding.

		- Make text content readable and understandable.
		- Make content appear and operate in predictable ways.
		- Help users avoid and correct mistakes.

	WCAG Principle: Robust
		Content must be robust enough that it can be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies. As technologies and users' tools evolve, the content should remain accessible.

		- Maximize compatibility with current and future user tools.
		- Correctly identify the name, role, and value of elements so they can be used by current and upcoming technologies.


What is UNiversal Design?
	The goal of universal design is to create products, services, and environments that meet the needs of everyone who uses them, minimizing or eliminating the need for adaptations or accommodations. Consider the fire alarm, which typically uses a piercing sound as its alert. Since people with hearing disabilities may not be able to hear the sound, the same alarm should also produce other alerts such as flashing lights, haptics, or text messages.

	When products, services, and environments are not designed for universal use, a person with a disability may need individual accommodations so they can participate and accomplish the same tasks as someone without a disability.

	The philosophy of universal design underlies or can be a synonym for:
	- inclusive design
	- design for all
	- human-centered design
	- lifespan design

	The principles of universal design were first developed by architect Ronald Mace:

	"The design of products and environments to be usable by all people, to the greatest extent possible, without the need for adaptation or specialized design"

	Built Environment Accessibility
		The goal of universal design for the built environment is to ensure the accessibility of:
		- buildings
		- public spaces
		- transportation systems

	Designing for accessibility saves costs
		When creating physical spaces, it is important to consider the needs of all people in the design. The cost of not integrating UD into the initial design and construction stages of a building is often greater than its overall construction cost.

	Rules and regulations
		Rules and regulations vary among countries. Many countries have minimum building standards for design and construction, but simply complying with minimum standards usually results in the need for accommodations. Countries often have best practice UD guidelines to support their minimum standard to better support accessibility.

		Some areas of focus in built environment accessibility include:
			- Access into and out of buildings
			- Inside buildings, including:
				- movement and evacuation
				- fixtures and furnishings
				- accessibility of facilities
				- signage
			- Transportation, including: 
				- access to different means of transportation
				- routes
				- the outdoor environment
			- Policies, guidelines, and recommendations

	Principle 1. Equitable Use
		The design is useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities.

		- Provide the same means of use for all users: identical whenever possible; equivalent when not.
		- Avoid segregating or stigmatizing any users.
		- Provisions for privacy, security, and safety should be equally available to all users.
		- Make the design appealing to all users.

		Examples
			Physical
				- Automated doors provide the same entry to people of all abilities.
				- Curb cuts in sidewalks.

			Digital
				- Providing closed captions on videos.
				- Using high contrast text.

	Principle 2. Flexibility in Use
		The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities.

		- Provide choice in methods of use.
		- Accommodate right or left-handed access and use.
		- Facilitate the user's accuracy and precision.
		- Provide adaptability to the user's pace.

		Examples
			Physical
				- Ambidextrous scissors can be used by both right- and left-handed people.
				- A moving walkway in an airport can be used for walking or standing.
			Digital
				- Customizable dashboards.
				- A form that allows a generous time to complete.

	Principle 3. Simple and Intuitive Use
		The use of the design is easy to understand, regardless of the user’s experience, knowledge, language skills, or current concentration level.
			- Eliminate unnecessary complexity.
			- Be consistent with user expectations and intuition.
			- Accommodate a wide range of literacy and language skills.
			- Arrange information consistent with its importance.

		Examples
			Physical
			- A cup with a handle.
			- IKEA instructions.

			Digital
			- The different parts of a web page are easy to locate and identify.
			- Navigation elements are consistent throughout a website.
			- Content is written in plain language.

	Principle 4: Perceptible Information
		The design communicates necessary information effectively to the user, regardless of ambient conditions or the user’s sensory abilities.

		- Use different modes (pictorial, verbal, tactile) for redundant presentation of essential information.
		- Provide adequate contrast between essential information.
		- Differentiate elements in ways that can be described.
		- Provide compatibility with a variety of techniques or devices used by people with sensory limitations.

		Examples
			Physical
				- Redundant cueing in airports like voice communication and signage.
				- Plastic credit and debit cards with cutouts to indicate which is which.

			Digital
				- Images provide contextually relevant alternative text.
				- Adequate color contrast.

	Principle 5: Tolerance for Error
		The design minimizes hazards and the adverse consequences of accidental or unintended actions.

		- Arrange elements to minimize hazards and errors.
		- Provide warnings of hazards and errors.
		- Provide fail-safe features.
		- Discourage unconscious action in tasks that require vigilance.

		Physical
			- A double-cut key is easily inserted into a recessed keyhole either way.
			- Power tools that prevent injuries.

		Digital
			- Supporting review and correction of information on a form before it is submitted.
			- Using date pickers to ensure proper date format is used.

	Principle 6: Low Physical Effort
		The design can be used efficiently and comfortably and with a minimum of fatigue.

		- Allow the user to maintain a neutral body position.
		- Use reasonable operating forces.
		- Minimize repetitive actions.

		Examples
			Physical
				- Lever or loop handles on doors and faucets.
				- Touch lamps operated without a switch.

			Digital
				- Voice activation.
				- Keyboard shortcuts to minimize using a mouse.

	Principle 7: Size & Approach for Use
		Appropriate size and space are provided for approach, reach, manipulation, and use regardless of the user’s body size, posture, or mobility.

		- Provide a clear line of sight to important elements for any seated or standing user.
		- Make reach to all components comfortable for any seated or standing user.
		- Accommodate variations in hand and grip size.
		- Provide adequate space for the use of assistive devices or personal assistance.

		Examples
			Physical
				- Wide gates at subway stations that accommodate all users.
				- ATMs at a reachable height for people who use wheelchairs.

			Digital
				- Large target areas, of at least 75 pixels, for buttons on mobile apps.
				- Large links and buttons, with plenty of space around them.
				- A website visually highlights the most important information.


	Universal Design for Learning
		The three principles of Universal Design for Learning. 

		Provide multiple means of engagement uses the brain's affective networks and addresses the "why" of learning. 

		Provide multiple means of representation uses the recognition networks and addresses the "what" of learning. 

		Provide multiple means of action and expression uses the strategic networks and addresses the "how" of learning.

		Overview:
		Universal design for learning (UDL) provides a framework for teaching that considers the diverse needs of learners. It is designed to remove learning barriers and create successful learners.

		UDL asks teachers to provide variety in:

		- Engaging and motivating learners.
		- Presenting information.
		- How learners demonstrate their knowledge.

		# UDL Principles and Guidelines
 
| | **Engagement** | **Representation** | **Action and Expression** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Brain Networks** | Affective Networks | Recognition Networks | Strategic Networks |
| **Answers the question** | The "why" of learning | The "what" of learning | The "how" of learning |
| **Goal** | Learners are purposeful and motivated | Learners are resourceful and knowledgeable | Learners are strategic and goal-directed |
| | **UDL Guidelines** | | |
| **Access** | Provide options for recruiting interest | Provide options for perception | Provide options for physical action |
| **Build** | Provide options for sustaining effort and persistence | Provide options for languages and symbols | Provide options for expression and communication |
| **Internalize** | Provide options for self regulation | Provide options for comprehension | Provide options for executive functions |

	# Multiple Means of Engagement

	Our minds have three broad learning networks:

		- Caring and prioritizing (Why am I learning this?)
		- Recognition (What am I learning?)
		- Skills and strategies (How can I learn this and show that I understand?)
	For students to be enthusiastic about learning, and gain knowledge and skills, learning activities must be designed to help them do all three.

	Learners vary in how they are motivated or engaged with learning. For example, some like spontaneity while others prefer routine.

	The principle of providing multiple means of engagement taps into the caring and prioritizing network, and creates motivation for learning by getting learners challenged, excited, or interested

	It answers the learner's question, "Why should I learn this?"

	Teachers should provide options to:

		- Spark excitement and curiosity for learning.
		- Tackle challenges with focus and determination.
		- Harness the power of emotions and motivation in learning.

	Examples: 
		- A choice of tasks so learners can find the challenges that interest them.
		- Activities that are culturally and socially relevant.
		- Activities that encourage self-reflection.

	# Multiple Means of Representation
		This principle taps into the mind's recognition network, and helps every learner perceive and make sense of the information being taught.

		It answers the learner's question, "What am I learning?"

		Learners differ in how they perceive and understand information. Those with disabilities or language or cultural differences in particular may need different ways of learning the information.

		Teachers should provide options for:

		- Information, so it doesn't depend on a single sense (sight, hearing, movement, or touch).
		- Communicating in ways that create a shared understanding.
		- Supporting comprehension.

	# Multiple Means of Action and Expression
		This principle taps into the skills and strategies network, and helps learners navigate the learning environment and express what they know.

		It answers the learner's question, "How can I learn this and show that I understand?"

		Learners vary in how they can navigate a learning environment and express their knowledge. For example, some may be able to express themselves well in writing but not in speech.

		Teachers should provide options for:

		- Accessible materials and tools.
		- Creating and sharing ideas using tools that help attain learning goals.
		- Developing and acting on plans to make the most of learning.

		Examples:
		- Multiple examples of novel solutions to a problem.
		- Posting learning goals, objectives, and schedules.
		- Providing options for students to show they understand a topic, for example by writing an article or by giving a talk.

	# International Declarations and Conventions on Disability Rights
		The purpose of the present Convention is to promote, protect and ensure the full and equal enjoyment of all human rights and fundamental freedoms by all persons with disabilities, and to promote respect for their inherent dignity.
			- Article 1: The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities

			Why have legal instruments?

			Legal instruments can address the rights of people with disabilities at different levels. They may:

			- Grant fundamental or human rights to every person.
			- Prohibit discrimination based on disability.
			- Directly address the rights of people with disabilities.
			- The 1999 Inter-American Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Persons with Disabilities provides a clear definition of disability discrimination:

			The term ‘discrimination against persons with disabilities’ means any distinction, exclusion, or restriction based on a disability, record of disability, condition resulting from a previous disability, or perception of disability, whether present or past, which has the effect or objective of impairing or nullifying the recognition, enjoyment, or exercise by a person with a disability of his or her human rights and fundamental freedoms.

		The Universal Declaration of Human Rights
			The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) was drafted by the United Nations General Assembly in Paris in 1948, and produced a common standard of achievement for all peoples and nations. It was written by representatives from all regions of the world.

			It is the first declaration that people have fundamental human rights that must be universally protected.

			The declaration does not specify people with disabilities, but they are understood to be included in the concepts of "everyone," "all," and "no one."

		The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities
			The United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) of 2006 is the first binding international human rights instrument that specifically protects the rights of people with disabilities. States that ratify (accept) the agreement must take steps to implement those rights.

			In its introduction to The CRPD, The United Nation's Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights states that:

				The Convention follows decades of work by the United Nations to change attitudes and approaches to persons with disabilities. It takes to a new height the movement from viewing persons with disabilities as “objects” of charity, medical treatment and social protection towards viewing persons with disabilities as “subjects” with rights, who are capable of claiming those rights and making decisions for their lives based on their free and informed consent as well as being active members of society.

				The Convention is intended as a human rights instrument with an explicit, social development dimension. It adopts a broad categorization of persons with disabilities and reaffirms that all persons with all types of disabilities must enjoy all human rights and fundamental freedoms. It clarifies and qualifies how all categories of rights apply to persons with disabilities and identifies areas where adaptations have to be made for persons with disabilities to effectively exercise their rights and areas where their rights have been violated, and where protection of rights must be reinforced.

			Article 9 of the CRPD: Accessibility
				Article 9 is concerned with accessibility. It begins:

				To enable persons with disabilities to live independently and participate fully in all aspects of life, States Parties shall take appropriate measures to ensure to persons with disabilities access, on an equal basis with others, to the physical environment, to transportation, to information and communications, including information and communications technologies and systems, and to other facilities and services open or provided to the public, both in urban and in rural areas.


		The Marrakest Treaty
			The purpose of the Marrakesh Treaty to Facilitate Access to Published Works for Persons who are Blind, Visually Impaired, or Otherwise Print Disabled is to ensure that people who have vision or other print disabilities have access to books and other printed materials.

			In its introduction to the treaty, the World Intellectual Property Organization states that:

			The Marrakesh Treaty was adopted in 2013 in Marrakesh.

			The Treaty was adopted in 2013, with the goal of ending the lack of books accessible to blind, visually impaired, and otherwise print disabled people. Since a major obstacle for making books available in accessible formats is copyright limitations, the Treaty creates a copyright exception. The exception allows the creation of accessible formats of copyrighted print materials for people with visual and other print disabilities. The treaty also allows the import and export of accessible format versions.

	Regional, National, and Domain-Specific Interests.
		- Disability Rights are Human Rights

		Civil rights protections for people with disabilities have spread rapidly across many countries since the 1990s. The United States' groundbreaking Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, which prohibits discrimination because of disability, provided the model for the United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD). In Europe, thirty-three countries have adopted some form of disability equality protections.

		Along with the CRPD, the shift from the medical to the social model of disability is a main driver of the legal shift from a welfare or social security law model towards anti-discrimination or equality law. Disability rights laws challenge segregation and exclusion, usually by requiring social institutions such as governments, corporations, and schools to reduce or eliminate discrimination against people with disabilities.

		Disability discrimination laws are considered a truly new development in disability policy around the world. Laws that treat disability as a discrimination category recognize that people with disabilities are people with rights, not people with problems.

		Some disability civil rights laws include guidelines or checklists for measuring accessibility in such areas as architecture and the built environment, employment and the workplace, educational settings, retail venues, hospitality, and entertainment.

		# Legal Approaches
			The law has two approaches to granting rights to or accommodating the needs of people with disabilities in practice, reasonable accommodation (also called individual accommodation) and accessibility.

			# Reasonable accommodation
			Reasonable accommodation aims to ensure equal treatment on a case-by-case basis. The European Union's Employment Equality Directive provides a good description for reasonable accommodation in employment:

			In order to guarantee compliance with the principle of equal treatment in relation to persons with disabilities, reasonable accommodation shall be provided. This means that employers shall take appropriate measures, where needed in a particular case, to enable a person with a disability to have access to, participate in, or advance in employment, or to undergo training, unless such measures would impose a disproportionate burden on the employer. This burden shall not be disproportionate when it is sufficiently remedied by measures existing within the framework of the disability policy of the Member State concerned.

			# Accessibility

			For accessibility, the conditions or requirements for using a product, service, or environment should be met regardless of a person’s disability status. Section 508 of the US Rehabilitation Act of 1973 follows this approach. It requires that federal departments and agencies developing, procuring, maintaining, or using electronic and information technology, ensure that the technology allows:

			individuals with disabilities who are members of the public seeking information or services from a Federal department or agency to have access to and use of information and data that is comparable to the access to and use of the information and data by such members of the public who are not individuals with disabilities.

	Applying Standards and Regulations to ICT
		ICT accessibility is a critical issue for people with disabilities.

			Most countries provide laws protecting the civil rights of disabled persons for homes, parks, businesses, and educational facilities. However, this has not been the case with information communication technology accessibility in all countries.

			This lesson covers ICT laws and standards in the United States and the European Union.

		US Laws and Standards
			# Section 508
			- In the United States, federal, state, and local government websites must meet Section 508. The 508 standard uses the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0 as the conformance standard.

			- Section 508 applies only to government websites.

			# Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

			The ADA does not include any legal standards for website accessibility owned by business or non-profit organizations. Nevertheless, businesses and other organizations can be sued for lack of accessibility.

			The ADA is enforced by the US Department of Justice, Civil Rights Division. In cases involving educational institutions, it is enforced by the US Department of Education, Office for Civil Rights.

			Title II
			Title II protects people with disabilities from discrimination in public places, including state and local government spaces and websites.

			Title III
			Title III prohibits discrimination based on disability in private places that welcome the public, including businesses, schools, places of recreation, offices, and medical buildings. E-commerce, organizational websites, and public mobile applications fall under Title III.

			Businesses and other organizations can be sued under the ADA for the inaccessibility of their e-commerce and public websites, and mobile apps.

			European Accessibility Act
				The European Accessibility Act came into full force in 2025. It applies to both the public and privates sectors. The act mandates functional requirements for products and services identified as most important for people with disabilities, and includes a comprehensive set of minimum requirements for private businesses to ensure their products and services are accessible.

			Accessibilty Statements
				Accessibility Statements
					Statements may be required, such as by the EU Web Accessibility Directive or the European Accessibility Act, or may be voluntary. 

				Web Accessibility Directive
					The directive affects the public sector. It requires public sector bodies to "provide and regularly update a ‘detailed, comprehensive and clear’ accessibility statement on their website or mobile application."

				European Accessibility Act
					The act affects the private sector. It requires private entities to include information assessing how their product or service meets accessibility requirements.

				Voluntary Accessibility Statements
					In instances where they are not required organizations may still wish to publish a statement, which can serve to to provide information on the accessibility of the product or service and demonstrate a commitment to accessibility. 

			Accessibility Conformance Reports
				Accessibility Conformance Reports
					Conformance reports document the accessibility of a website or application against standards such as:

					- WCAG
					- EN 301 549 (specific to European Union)
					- Section 508 (specific to public entities in the United States)
					- They are used to demonstrate compliance with laws, in the ICT procurement process, and for internal reporting and product development.

					Voluntary Product Accessibility Template (VPAT)
						The VPAT is a commonly used template for documenting how well an ICT product meets accessibility standards. It is used internationally by both buyers and sellers. In the same way organizations require information security compliance reports, many require a VPAT as part of their procurement process.

	The Importance of Management Champions
		Launching and sustaining an accessibility program requires champions from key areas across the organization. It is necessary to build champions’ understanding of accessibility so they can both understand the levels of accessibility within their area of responsibility and lead initiatives needed to improve capabilities.

		Champions within the organization become advocates during the accessibility adoption plan and help sustain efforts by engaging their teams, building awareness and skills, and evangelizing accessibility. Champions help:

		- Build a vision and align implementation strategies
		- Sustain ongoing commitment and collaboration
		- Support the programmatic integration of accessibility throughout organizational processes, making clear the distinction between it and discreet accessibility projects
		- Lead the adoption of an accessibility maturity model

	Evaluating for Accessibility:
		Testing is critical to the design and development of accessible ICT, and practices must be built into an organization’s accessibility approach. The W3C recommends evaluating early and throughout the design and development process. It is easier and less costly to find and address accessibility issues early.

		The following recommendations are compiled from recommendations by the W3C and other organizations.

		- Ensure the product or service is fit for its purpose.
		- Design for usability by people with disabilities.
		- Use a born accessible approach to design.
		- Create reusable design and code libraries that help developers code for accessibility.
		- Use quality assurance tools, methods, and protocols including accessibility evaluation tools. Do not rely solely on automated checkers, as they only catch about 25% of defects. Human evaluation is critical to ensuring the product or service created is accessible.
		- Perform formative, summative, and continuous evaluations. Test throughout the project lifecycle and any time new content is added or code is updated.
		- Include people with disabilities in evaluations.
		- If there are gaps in your organization’s capabilities, use outside expert analysis and consultation until you build up in-house expertise.

	Recruiting and Hiring
		Recruiting and Including People with Disabilities
			- Include your organization’s disability inclusion statement in job postings and on the recruitment pages of your website.
			- Post job openings on disability-focused job boards
			- Ensure your organization’s recruitment website and all postings are accessible
			- Promote your organization at disability-focused job fairs
			- Ensure your facilities are accessible to applicants and employees, and that you provide reasonable accommodations including assistive technologies
			- Educate all employees, especially managers, on working with employees with disabilities
			- Ensure hiring and supervising managers understand their obligations for accessibility and the organizational benefits that an employee with disabilities brings to the workplace

				- Ability to use and test with screen reader software

		Communication Management Strategies
			Ensure the messages your organization delivers internally and externally are accessible. Your organization should:
				- Publish accessibility standards for communications
				- Provide training to communicators on using people first and plain language
				- Ensure documents are accessible
				- Ensure websites and all the media types it contains or references are accessible
				- Caption and describe videos and other time-based communications
				- Design for how people use assistive technologies

		Legal and Public Relations Implications
			Organizations may have legal obligations to ensure their products and services, and information about them, are accessible to people with disabilities. Further, there are positive public relations gains to be had, as the market for accessible solutions is large and customer satisfaction is directly tied to a product's success.

			In a large organization, public relations staff should consult with legal or risk and compliance before publishing accessibility information about the organization.

			In small organizations, people in roles of responsibility should seek guidance from laws and policies.

			Recommendations:

			- Become familiar with relevant standards and laws
			- Assess your organization’s legal liability
			- Ensure the organization has and is following an ICT accessibility plan
			- Ensure the plan includes documenting how products and services meet accessibility standards
			- Capitalize on the positive public relations that accessibility brings to your organization

		Procurement Best Practices
			Purchasing ICT solutions that are usable by everyone in your organization and by your customers is a key component of an ICT accessibility program. There are numerous benefits of procuring accessible products. Among others, products that are designed for universal use cost less over time since they do not require adaptation or redesign; they are a large factor in providing a welcoming and inclusive environment, improving productivity since they are more usable, and reducing legal risk.

			Setting accessibility requirements when purchasing a product or service, or when contracting a supplier to develop or design something from scratch, is important. Large companies or groups of companies, as well as public sector agencies who procure handle large budgets, which means that well-developed accessibility requirements may impact large parts of the market.

			Best practices include:

			- Verifying product accessibility claims
			- Verifying a vendor’s accessibility expertise and capacity
			- Requiring product accessibility in contractual agreements
			- Periodically reviewing a vendor’s product accessibility roadmap for adherence
			- Leveraging the organization’s procurement policies and requirements to influence vendors
